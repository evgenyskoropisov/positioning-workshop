# Mercuryo.io: 02 Market Pressure Map

## Lane 1: Direct competitors

| Company | Buyer | Promise | Proof signal | Tradeoff | Weakness |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| MoonPay | Consumer-facing crypto users and brands that want a recognizable buy/sell layer | Fast and simple crypto buying and selling with broad payment-method support | Homepage centers buying and selling crypto with cards, bank transfers, Apple Pay, and PayPal | Strong brand and ease story, but more retail-coded | Consumer-first positioning may underplay enterprise "engine" value |
| Transak | Product and developer teams in web3 apps | Simple fiat on/off ramp integration for web3 and crypto apps | Homepage leads with developer integration and coverage across many assets and countries | Clear integration story and broad coverage | More strongly associated with ramps than with a broader payments engine |
| Ramp Network | Wallet and app teams plus end users | Buy and sell crypto without leaving the app or wallet | Homepage claims web3 financial infrastructure, single verification, and 8 million customers | Strong embedded-flow promise and ease story | Broader web3 finance story can still feel consumer-transaction-led rather than partner-platform-led |

## Lane 2: Indirect alternatives

| Option | Buyer | Promise | Proof signal | Tradeoff | Weakness |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Traditional PSP plus card and bank-rail vendors | Ops and finance teams | Familiar payments infrastructure with known vendors | Existing enterprise payment relationships | Better for fiat payments, weak for web3-native flows | Does not solve crypto-specific rails, wallets, or compliance complexity |
| Hosted exchange or wallet redirect flow | Product teams shipping quickly | Launch fast by sending users to an existing buy or sell experience | Easy to implement and low engineering lift | Fastest path to market | Breaks brand continuity and leaks users out of the core product |
| Single-product specialist vendor | Team with one immediate need | Solve only on-ramp, only off-ramp, or only cards | Focused vendor expertise | Simpler near-term decision | Creates stack sprawl if the company expands payment use cases later |

## Lane 3: Manual workflow or internal team

| Option | Why buyers choose it | Hidden cost | Why it persists |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Build and manage payment stack in-house | Maximum control over UX, economics, and compliance choices | Slow launch, higher regulatory burden, fragmented integrations, ongoing ops load | Leadership believes payments are core enough to own directly |
| Stitch multiple regional or use-case vendors | Best-of-breed flexibility and coverage by market | Hard orchestration, inconsistent UX, vendor management overhead | Feels lower risk than committing to one broader platform |

## Lane 4: No-decision

| Reason for delay | Trigger missing | Risk of doing nothing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Current on-ramp or redirect works "well enough" | No visible revenue or retention pain yet | User leakage and lower monetization continue quietly |
| Compliance and geography concerns feel too risky | Clear internal owner and rollout plan | Competitors ship better in-product payment experiences first |
| Team sees cards or off-ramp as future problems | Immediate pressure is only on acquisition | Company locks itself into a narrow vendor setup that is hard to expand later |

## Pattern summary

- Table-stakes claims: easy buy and sell, embedded flow, broad coverage, secure and compliant operations, support for wallets and apps
- Overused category language: web3 payments, financial infrastructure, seamless, on-ramp, off-ramp, global, compliant
- Claims with weak proof: seamless enterprise solutions gateway, most trusted compliance stack, payment engine without a specific outcome
- White space worth testing: one embedded payment engine for web3 businesses across buy, sell, and spend, instead of a patchwork of separate ramps and card vendors
- Why buyers may stay put: switching costs, compliance fear, current vendor inertia, and unclear ownership of payments inside the product team

## Working summary

- Settled: Mercuryo competes in a crowded field where "embedded crypto payments" is table stakes.
- Uncertain: Whether buyers perceive Mercuryo as a broader platform or still primarily as an on-ramp vendor.
- Decision to force next: Position Mercuryo around one business outcome that direct competitors do not own as cleanly.
