# IncidentBridge: 03 Route Scorecard

## Route 1: Proof-led

- Best for: Skeptical engineering leaders who need fast ROI justification.
- Promise: Cut investigation time materially without replacing existing observability tools.
- Proof: Early pilots show a 42 percent median reduction in investigation time.
- What it excludes: Broad platform story and ambitious category creation.
- Main risk: Sounds useful but less memorable.
- Confidence: High.

## Route 2: Wedge-led

- Best for: Product and platform teams that already feel dashboard fatigue during incidents.
- Promise: Build the causal timeline that existing observability and incident tools do not provide.
- Proof: Paid pilots and design partner feedback show strong demand for one cross-tool incident thread.
- What it excludes: Full incident response orchestration and full observability replacement.
- Main risk: Requires careful explanation so buyers do not confuse it with adjacent categories.
- Confidence: High.

## Route 3: Category-led

- Best for: Buyers who want a bigger strategic story about reliability workflows.
- Promise: Become the incident intelligence layer between alerting and postmortem.
- Proof: Directional only; category is still emerging.
- What it excludes: Clear near-term focus.
- Main risk: Too broad for current proof and stage.
- Confidence: Medium.

## Comparison

| Route | Believability | Differentiation | Ease to repeat | Proof burden |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Proof-led | High | Medium | High | Medium |
| Wedge-led | High | High | Medium | Medium |
| Category-led | Medium | High | Medium | High |

## Recommendation

- Best default route: Wedge-led.
- Why: It gives IncidentBridge a clear reason to exist without pretending to replace observability or incident response tooling.
- What must be true for it to work: The product must keep proving that causal reconstruction is the bottleneck buyers feel most.
- What to watch out for: Avoid vague AI language and avoid drifting into a full-platform pitch.
